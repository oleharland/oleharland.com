Remote working in an enterprise company outside their timezone

I’ve always been working remote for some short time. Most of the time two to three days; a full week at most. And always in the same time zone as my colleagues in the Hamburg office. Many people outside of my company work asynchronously and remotely on a day to day bases. Some of those companies don’t even have an office and are working completely remote. I always wanted to try out to do it for a longer period. I had the plan to travel to Asia again and picked Bali as a perfect place for my experiment. So I agreed on a two weeks remote trip combined with some vacation.

Preparation

In advanced I prepared some things to make this work.

  1. I took a look at my day to day schedule. If some meetings were impossible due to the time shift I proposed new times. Unfortunately, this also meant that I needed to accept that I would not be able to do product discoveries or workshops.
  2. I knew from my other remote experiences that working in a coworking space would help to concentrate. I researched my options and decided for the Dojo in Canggu. They have the best internet connection on the island and also have a little café with food.
  3. I had to talk to a few people to tell them about my idea and get them convinced. My Product Owner and my team had to be on board since I would work with them on a day to day base. Since we did this already for a few days and a week there was no problem for them.
  4. I started communicating to colleagues that I’m not in the office but still reachable. I also added a note in my office calendar saying “working elsewhere” to make sure everyone gets it.

Arriving in Bali

We planned for the first week in Bali some vacation. This was a good idea since we were pretty jet lagged. We took that time not only to surf but also to make us aware of the surroundings, getting SIM cards and looked for a close Airbnb near the co-working space.

As the first remote week started we tried to run on a 4pm to 12pm schedule. Pretty soon we were exhausted by working that late and adjusted it to 2pm to 10pm what in German time is 7am to 3pm. So we were starting pretty early but had enough overlap time for meetings and if necessary we stayed longer.
Having a ticket system where you can pull new tickets is crucial. You work sometimes out of sync with your colleagues and you can’t get easily in touch with them. With this you should also pay attention to new upcoming tickets. Ask relevant questions early so that if you pull a ticket all information is already documented.

Meetings are a bit more complicated when you’re remote. We’re having our standup’s in our office and you’re now the guy on the screen. If somebody now is saying “I did this with him” with a little gesture pointing at somebody it’s hard to recognise who he/she meant. There are already couple of remote guidelines from experienced teams like trello that are solving this. Trello has the policy of “If one is remote, everybody has to be remote”. What this means is no matter how many people are in the office they all use their own computer and login to the conference as they were remote. This wasn’t an option for me because this is not the normal case at the moment.

Sometimes people are forgetting you or didn’t prepare important meeting material that remote people can access. I often hear from coworker that when I say I’m in home office or remote that they want to postpone the meeting instead of doing a video call.

You have to push for a direct conversation via HipChat or video call instead of waiting days or some weeks to be back in the office.

Providing the right tools with a few guidelines can help with this. Make sure you have good headphones to prevent bad audio quality. Choose the right place with good internet connection to make sure you don’t get interrupted. And keep your video on all the time to show your engagement.

Conclusion

Remote work is not for everybody. You have to drive yourself towards work and blend out everything else like palms, beaches and surfing.

Communication is the key to successful remote working and being able to present your work remotely with the right tools. Have a backup plan if anything is breaking. But no matter how good you’re prepared it not only depends on you. Your coworkers are deciding on how well this will work out. They have to prepare everything remote friendly and have to set up or book virtual conference rooms. Even if they want to talk to you, you have to make them feel comfortable to just call you instead of coming by your desk.

We all should develop a mindset of remote work and make it possible for to people who want to work remote to do it so. But keep in mind for some tasks like product discoveries and team building it’s better to be there in person.
